---

## Bottom line up front

Two new pieces of code landed: Phase 5 causal patching (`eval/causal.py`) and
the destroyed-structure control (`--shuffle_routes` in
`data/prepare_city.py`). The destroyed-structure control produced an
**unexpected result that forces a reinterpretation** of what we have. The
short version:

- The shuffled-trained model's probe achieves **higher** R² than the
  real-trained model's, on every split.
- This is because **route SET-MEMBERSHIP carries strong geographic signal
  even when token ORDER is random** — geographically-close nodes co-occur
  in the same "bag of route members" regardless of the order they were
  traversed in.
- Therefore the geographic clustering in activations does NOT specifically
  arise from sequence learning. The thing that DOES require sequence
  training is graph-adjacency knowledge: the real model assigns 98%
  probability mass to a node's real neighbors; the shuffled model assigns 6%.

The "emergent metric map" framing of the project needs to be split into
two distinct phenomena: **geographic clustering** (cheap, emerges from any
geographic co-occurrence training) and **graph adjacency** (the actual
sequence-learned representation). The Phase 5 causal experiment as designed
doesn't yet distinguish these, so the strong "the model uses its location
map" claim is not supported by the data on hand.

This is, on net, an interesting result. It tightens up what's defensible
and reveals a methodological caveat that interpretability work on
graph-structured data should generally know about.

## What was implemented this session

### `eval/probe.py` — node-level split

Added the **node-level train/test split** alongside the standard
position-level split. Probe is trained on 80% of unique TOKEN ids and
tested on the held-out 20%. A probe that learned a per-token lookup
fails this split; a probe that learned a continuous geographic map
holds up.

Detected the **MLP-probe lookup-contamination** issue earlier in the
session: untrained MLP probes get near-perfect R² on the position-level
split (because there are only 663 unique tokens and the MLP can fit
their random-vector → coord mappings), but collapse to ~0 on the
node-level split. The trained MLP also drops on the node-level split,
just less dramatically. **This means the standard "linear ≈ MLP →
linearly encoded" criterion from Nanda 2023 cannot be applied cleanly
in our continuous-target, few-tokens setting.**

Ran on all three cities (London, Manhattan, Boston) with consistent
results across the board. Cross-city table is in
`PROGRESS_2026-05-24_evening.md`.

### `eval/causal.py` — Phase 5 (NEW)

Activation patching at a chosen residual-stream layer. For each test
position where the model is at node A:

1. Cache the residual stream at the chosen layer.
2. Train a linear probe at that layer (W: 2 × n_embd → location).
3. Pick a random target node B.
4. Compute the minimum-norm patch:
   `Δ = pinv(W) · (coords[B] - W·a)` — the smallest perturbation in
   activation space that makes the probe decode B's coords from `a + Δ`.
5. Run three forward passes:
   - unpatched
   - target-patched (residual gets `Δ` added at the chosen layer)
   - random-patched (a random direction with the same L2 magnitude as `Δ`)
6. Score the next-token distribution: probability mass on A's graph
   neighbors and on B's graph neighbors.

Added `--patch_scale` to scale the pseudoinverse patch, since the
unscaled patch typically has L2 norm 7–13× the activation's L2 (the
probe is ill-conditioned: small singular values in the location subspace
inflate patches). Smaller scales undershoot the location shift but
disturb the rest of the residual less.

The script also prints `‖patch‖ / ‖activation‖` so anyone running it can
see how aggressive the patch is.

### `data/prepare_city.py` — `--shuffle_routes` flag (NEW)

Phase 4 destroyed-structure control: after each route is generated by
shortest-path or random-walk traversal, randomly permute the INTERIOR
tokens (BOS, the source node, the destination node stay fixed). The
SET of nodes in each route is unchanged; the order is destroyed.

A model trained on these shuffled routes cannot learn directed
adjacency (the next token in a route no longer corresponds to a real
graph successor). Predicted behavior: it would learn token-frequency
statistics and route-membership statistics, but its representations
should NOT cluster geographically — because there's no
sequence-prediction reason for them to.

That prediction turned out to be wrong (see below).

## What happened when we ran the destroyed-structure control

We generated `data/london_shuffled/` (same flags as the real London
corpus, just with `--shuffle_routes` added) and trained an identical
`small.py` model on it. Two things came out:

### Outcome 1 (as expected): The model cannot learn the graph

| Metric | Real London | Shuffled London |
|---|---:|---:|
| Val perplexity | 1.65 | **24.98** |
| Gen perplexity | 2.13 | **28.32** |
| P(A's neighbors) unpatched at L3 | 0.984 | **0.063** |

The shuffled model is dramatically worse at next-token prediction, and
its probability mass on a node's actual graph neighbors at the
intervention test positions is only 6.3% (vs 98.4% for the real model).
So the graph-adjacency aspect of training was destroyed cleanly.

### Outcome 2 (surprising): The probe gets HIGHER R² on the shuffled model

| Probe metric | Real London | **Shuffled London** | Comment |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Position-level linear R² (best) | 0.867 (embed) | **0.982 (embed)** | shuffled higher |
| Position-level MLP R² (best) | 0.998 | 0.9995 | both saturated |
| **Node-level linear R² (best)** | 0.639 (L3) | **0.965 (L2)** | shuffled MUCH higher |
| **Node-level MLP R² (best)** | 0.775 (L3) | **0.992 (L2)** | shuffled MUCH higher |
| Untrained linear R² (node-level) | −0.05 | −0.05 | both at floor |

The shuffled model's activations are MORE linearly decodable to
(lat, lon) than the real model's. And this holds even on the node-level
split — the probe generalizes to unseen tokens just as well or better
on the shuffled model.

### Why this happens

The shuffling operation I implemented randomized the order WITHIN each
route, but preserved the SET of nodes in each route. And the set of
nodes in a route is itself geographically coherent — a real shortest
path from A to B traverses a specific neighborhood of intersections,
and the BAG of those intersections (without order) is enough to learn
"these intersections tend to appear together → they should have similar
embeddings."

In fact the shuffled task is closer to a *pure clustering objective*
than the real-graph task: the model can't learn ordered adjacency, so
it dedicates more representational capacity to clustering nodes by
co-occurrence. That clustering ends up tightly aligned with geographic
proximity (because co-occurrence is geographic), so the probe finds it
even more clearly than on the real model.

### Implication for the Phase 5 causal result

Earlier in the session we ran Phase 5 on real London at L3 and saw
target_beats_random in **79.5%** of positions — directional evidence
that patching toward B's location shifts predictions toward B's
neighbors more than a random-direction patch does. When we ran the same
experiment on the shuffled-trained model, target_beats_random was
**75.0%** — essentially the same.

So the current causal-patching design is not isolating "use of the
graph-adjacency representation." It's measuring "the model is sensitive
to patches in directions identified by the probe," which is true for
ANY model whose activations cluster geographically — including the
shuffled-trained model that cannot route at all.

**The strong Phase 5 causal claim is not supported by the current
data.** The 79.5% on real London is real, but its echo on the shuffled
model (75.0%) tells us the experiment isn't testing what we wanted.

## The reframe: two distinct emergent phenomena

What this session has surfaced is that **what we were calling "the
emergent map" is actually two separable phenomena**, with different
sources and different significance:

| Phenomenon | Real London | Shuffled London | Required training signal |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Geographic clustering of embeddings (probe R²) | high (0.64–0.87) | **higher** (0.97) | Just route co-occurrence (set-membership). Sequence order not needed. |
| Graph adjacency knowledge (P(A's neighbors), valid-edge rate) | very high (0.98 / 99.7%) | very low (0.06 / ~few %) | Sequence-order training on the real graph. |

The valid-edge / P(A's neighbors) metric is the cleanest separator we
have. It is the only thing in our pipeline that demonstrably requires
*sequence-prediction training on real routes* to emerge.

The probe-decoded clustering is real, but it is a property of *any*
training signal that has geographic co-occurrence — including
permutation-invariant bag-of-tokens objectives. That makes it a less
specific finding than the project's original framing implied.

## What is defensibly established by the data after this session

| Rung | Claim | Status |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The model learns the city graph (predicts real neighbors at 99.7%) | **Verified, clean** — only the real-trained model achieves this. |
| 2 | The model's activations contain a linearly-decodable geographic representation | Verified — but with the new caveat that the source of this is *any* geographic co-occurrence in training data, NOT specifically sequence learning. |
| 3 | This representation emerged from training (vs random init) | Verified — trained-vs-untrained linear-probe gap is large in all conditions tested. |
| 4 | The representation is *encoded linearly* | Not cleanly testable in our setting (MLP probe contaminated by lookup; documented in `PROGRESS_2026-05-24_evening.md`). |
| 5 | The representation *causally drives* next-hop predictions | **Not supported by current data.** Target-patch vs random-patch signal looks the same on the destroyed-structure model as on the real model. |
| 5′ | The model uses graph-adjacency knowledge for next-hop prediction | **Strongly suggested** by P(A's neighbors)=0.984 (real) vs 0.063 (shuffled), though this is correlational with training data, not a causal-intervention demonstration. |

## What I'd do next, given the new understanding

1. **A stronger destroyed-structure control: globally shuffle tokens across the entire training stream**, breaking even the set-membership co-occurrence signal. Predict: probe finally fails on this. ~30 min compute + small flag in `prepare_city.py`.

2. **An activation-transplant-style Phase 5**: instead of pseudoinverse-direction patches (which operate on the probe's clustering geometry and thus appear in any clustered model), take a real residual `a_B` from a position where the model genuinely processes node B and substitute it for `a_A`. This uses the model's *own* representation, bypassing the probe-direction contamination. Standard mech-interp practice.

3. **Either accept the reframed findings as the publishable result** (this is a workshop-paper-shaped contribution about methodological caveats in next-token-graph interpretability), or push for a stronger causal claim via the steps above.

The reframed story is actually more interesting than the original, because it
separates two phenomena that the prior framing conflated, and identifies a
specific methodological caveat (within-route shuffle does NOT destroy
geographic structure when set-membership is geographically coherent).
